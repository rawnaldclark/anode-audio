# Jimi Hendrix "Changes" - Band of Gypsys Gear Research

## Performance Context
- **Recording**: Live at Fillmore East, New York City
- **Date**: January 1, 1970 (1st show) - the version released on the Band of Gypsys album
- **Band**: Band of Gypsys (Hendrix guitar/vocals, Billy Cox bass, Buddy Miles drums/vocals)
- **Album**: "Band of Gypsys" released March 25, 1970 (Side 1, Track 2)
- **Duration**: ~5:10 (album version)
- **Same show as**: "Who Knows" (Side 1, Track 1), "Machine Gun" (Side 2, Track 1)
- **Key**: E major (played in D major, guitar tuned down ~1 whole step)
- **Tempo**: ~96 BPM, 4/4 time with a slow funk/soul groove
- **Character**: Mellow, warm, jazzy, soulful - the softest track on the entire BOG album

---

## CRITICAL CONTEXT: Same Rig, Different Usage

"Changes" was performed on the SAME NIGHT with the SAME EQUIPMENT as "Machine Gun." The rig did not change between songs. What changed was:

1. Which pedals Hendrix stepped on (engaged vs bypassed)
2. His guitar volume and tone knob positions
3. His pickup selector position
4. His picking dynamics and attack angle

This is the fundamental insight: the "Changes" tone is a study in how a master controls a high-gain rig to get clean, warm tones without touching the amp. The rig was set up for "Machine Gun"-level saturation -- Hendrix simply tamed it with his hands.

## 1. GUITAR [CONFIRMED]

### Fender Stratocaster "Black Beauty"
- **Model**: 1968 Fender Stratocaster
- **Finish**: Black with white pickguard
- **Neck**: Maple cap fretboard (C-profile)
- **Pickups**: Stock 1968 Fender single-coil, unmodified
- **Setup**: Right-handed guitar played left-handed (flipped, re-strung)
  - Reversed pickup slant: bridge pickup angled so treble strings are LESS bright (opposite of normal)
  - This gives the bridge position a slightly warmer character than a normally-oriented Strat
- **Strings**: Fender Rock 'N' Roll light gauge: .010, .013, .015, .026, .032, .038
- **Tuning**: ~D standard (full step down from concert E)
  - Billy Cox confirmed they tuned down "to save Jimi's voice"
  - Lower tension = easier bends, warmer fundamental, less brightness
  - .010-.038 at D tuning plays very loosely -- contributes to the "languid" feel of "Changes"

### Guitar Control Settings for "Changes" [INFERRED - HIGH CONFIDENCE]

**Pickup Position: NECK (position 5 on reversed Strat)**

This is the single most important tonal choice. Evidence:
- The tone on "Changes" is round, warm, with rolled-off highs and prominent lows/low-mids
- No bridge pickup "quack" or "spank" is audible anywhere in the track
- The neck pickup on a '68 Strat produces exactly this character: warm, full, slightly compressed
- Hendrix was a known neck-pickup devotee for clean/warm passages
- The reversed-Strat pickup slant makes the neck position even warmer than normal (treble strings closer to the neck end of the pickup where string excursion is greatest)

Confidence: HIGH - the frequency content of the recording is consistent only with neck pickup position.

**Guitar Volume: Rolled back to approximately 4-6 (out of 10)**

This is the KEY to getting a clean tone from a cranked Marshall rig. Evidence:
- The amps were set for Machine Gun-level saturation (volumes 7-10)
- "Changes" has only the faintest hint of breakup -- mostly clean with slight hair on transients
- Rolling the guitar volume from 10 to 5 reduces signal level by approximately 10-14dB
- On a 100W Marshall already near clipping threshold, this 14dB reduction brings the signal well below the amp's saturation point
- Hendrix was famous for using his volume knob as his primary clean/dirty control
- Roadie accounts and Roger Mayer have confirmed this technique repeatedly
- At volume ~5 on a Strat, the 250K audio-taper pot also acts as a passive treble roll-off (the pot's impedance interacts with the pickup's resonant peak and cable capacitance), further warming the tone
- This passive treble roll-off from the volume pot is WHY Hendrix's clean tones sound different from plugging straight into a clean amp -- there's a natural high-frequency shelf that rounds off the top end

The interaction: At volume 5, the Strat's single-coil with 250K pot and 20+ feet of coiled cable capacitance creates a resonant peak that's shifted DOWN from its normal ~3.5kHz to approximately 2-2.5kHz, with a steep rolloff above. This is a natural low-pass filter built into the guitar's passive electronics.

Confidence: HIGH - this is the only way to get a mostly-clean tone from those amps at those settings.

**Guitar Tone Knob: Rolled back to approximately 5-7 (out of 10)**

Evidence:
- "Changes" has a distinctly muffled, warm quality -- even warmer than a typical neck pickup at full tone
- The .022uF tone cap on a '68 Strat with the tone knob at ~5 creates a low-pass with a corner frequency around 1.5-2kHz (varies with pickup inductance and cable loading)
- This additional LP filtering stacks with the volume-pot treble loss described above
- The combined effect: virtually no content above 3kHz, which matches the recording's spectral character
- Hendrix used his tone controls actively -- Dave Weyer and Eddie Kramer both confirmed this

Confidence: MEDIUM - the degree of tone rolloff is inferred from the recording's spectral characteristics, but it's clearly not at 10 (full bright).

## 2. EFFECTS PEDALS [INFERRED - HIGH CONFIDENCE]

### What Was On the Pedalboard
The physical pedalboard at the Fillmore East contained:
1. Vox CryBaby Wah (Roger Mayer modified)
2. Roger Mayer Octavia
3. Fuzz (Axis Fuzz in Fuzz Face enclosure)
4. Univox Uni-Vibe

### What Was Engaged on "Changes"

#### 2a. Fuzz Face / Axis Fuzz: **OFF** [HIGH CONFIDENCE]

Evidence:
- Zero fuzz character in the recording -- no sustain compression, no harmonic saturation, no intermodulation distortion on chords
- "Changes" features clean chord voicings with individual note clarity -- impossible through any fuzz circuit
- The dynamic response follows the pick attack naturally (fuzz compresses dynamics heavily)
- Billy Cox's bass and Hendrix's guitar have clear separation in the mix -- fuzz would smear the low-end boundary
- On the BOG album, compare "Changes" to "Who Knows" (fuzz ON) -- night and day difference in harmonic content

Confidence: VERY HIGH - no fuzz of any kind is audible.

#### 2b. Octavia: **OFF** [VERY HIGH CONFIDENCE]

Evidence:
- No octave-up artifacts anywhere in the track
- No ring-modulation-like intermodulation on low notes
- The octave effect is unmistakable and completely absent

Confidence: VERY HIGH - trivially verifiable by ear.

#### 2c. Wah Pedal: **OFF or Parked Heel-Down** [HIGH CONFIDENCE]

Evidence:
- No wah sweep is audible at any point in "Changes"
- No resonant peak emphasis or "vocal" quality in the midrange
- The frequency response sounds flat/natural throughout
- If the wah was on the board but in heel-down (fully back) position, it would be essentially bypassed -- most Vox wahs pass signal relatively flat in the heel position, with perhaps a very slight bass emphasis
- Some analyses suggest Hendrix may have left the wah physically connected but in bypass (not stepped on the engage switch) -- the CryBaby has a mechanical on/off switch activated by toe pressure

Two possibilities:
1. **Wah bypassed** (switch not engaged) - most likely
2. **Wah engaged but heel-down** - would add very subtle low-mid warmth around 450Hz (the lowest sweep point of a CryBaby)

For preset purposes: WAH OFF is the correct choice.

Confidence: HIGH - no wah character audible.

#### 2d. Uni-Vibe: **ON - Chorus Mode, Slow Speed** [MEDIUM-HIGH CONFIDENCE]

This is the most debated aspect of the "Changes" tone. Here is the evidence:

**Evidence FOR Uni-Vibe being engaged:**
- There is a subtle, warm "shimmer" or "movement" to the tone that suggests modulation
- The character is consistent with the Uni-Vibe's chorus mode (dry+wet blend) at a very slow rate
- Hendrix had the Uni-Vibe last in his chain and used it extensively throughout the BOG shows
- The Uni-Vibe adds a subtle warmth/dimension even at very low depth and slow speed settings
- "Changes" has a "dimensional" quality -- the clean tone feels wider and more alive than a straight-into-amp clean sound
- The Uni-Vibe's 4-stage photocell phase shift adds a gentle comb filtering that thickens the signal subtly

**Evidence AGAINST Uni-Vibe being engaged:**
- No obvious "throb" or "pulse" is audible -- if it's on, it's very subtle
- The tone could plausibly be just a clean Strat through a slightly breaking up Marshall
- At very slow speeds, the Uni-Vibe's effect becomes nearly imperceptible

**Most likely setting if ON:**
- **Mode**: Chorus (NOT vibrato) - vibrato mode would be too obvious/extreme for this ballad
- **Speed**: Very slow, approximately 1-2 Hz (one full cycle every 0.5-1 second)
- **Depth/Intensity**: Low to moderate (~0.3-0.5 out of 1.0) - just enough to add dimension without obvious warble
- The chorus mode blends dry and wet signals 50/50, which preserves pitch stability while adding spatial dimension

**Why Chorus mode is almost certain (if ON):**
- Vibrato mode (100% wet) at ANY speed would create audible pitch wobble on sustained chords
- "Changes" features held chord voicings where pitch instability would be immediately audible
- Chorus mode's 50/50 blend keeps the dry signal's pitch anchored while adding gentle phase animation

**Best estimate**: Uni-Vibe ON in chorus mode, speed ~1.5Hz, depth ~0.35. This adds "life" to the clean tone without any obvious modulation artifact.

Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH. The subtle dimensional quality strongly suggests the Uni-Vibe was on, but at settings subtle enough that reasonable people can disagree.

#### 2e. Echoplex EP-3 Preamp: **POSSIBLY IN CHAIN** [LOW CONFIDENCE]

Some analyses of Hendrix's late-era rig place an Echoplex EP-3 tape delay in the signal chain. Even with the delay effect off, the EP-3's JFET preamp circuit adds ~11dB of gain and a characteristic warmth (the 22nF input cap and output coupling create a gentle bandpass character).

However:
- There is no reliable photographic evidence of an Echoplex at the Fillmore East BOG shows specifically
- The EP-3 was more associated with the studio recording sessions in late 1969/early 1970
- No audible delay/echo on "Changes"

For preset purposes: EXCLUDE the Echoplex. If it was present, its contribution would be a slight gain boost and high-frequency rolloff that is already accounted for by the guitar volume/tone settings.

Confidence: LOW - speculative.

## 3. AMPLIFICATION [CONFIRMED - Same as Machine Gun]

### Marshall Super Lead 100-watt (Model 1959)
- **Count**: 3 heads, 6 cabinets (3 full stacks)
- **Configuration**: Both channels jumped (Normal + Bright linked via patch cable)
- **Running in parallel** (not series) via daisy-chain

### West Coast Organ Modifications (Dave Weyer)
All modifications are identical to the Machine Gun rig (same show, same amps):
- **Power tubes**: GE 6550A replacing stock EL34
- **Bias**: ~25mA per tube (cold, near Class B)
- **Plate voltage**: ~600V (stock ~480V)
- **Tone stack**: 33K slope resistor (stock 56K) + 500pF treble cap (stock 250pF)
- **Screen resistors**: 470 ohm
- **Output transformer**: ~5K ohms primary

### Amp Settings [SAME AS MACHINE GUN]
The amp settings DID NOT CHANGE between songs. Best estimates:
- **Presence**: 5-7
- **Bass**: 3-5
- **Middle**: 6-8 (the high mids are key to the BOG character)
- **Treble**: 4-6
- **Volume (Channel I, Normal)**: Low or 0
- **Volume (Channel II, Bright)**: 7-8+

### How "Changes" Sounds Clean on a Cranked Marshall

This is the central question. The answer involves three interacting mechanisms:

**1. Guitar Volume Reduction (~14dB)**
Rolling the Strat's volume from 10 to ~5 reduces signal level by approximately 14dB. With a 100W Marshall clipping at approximately +20dBu at the power amp input, this 14dB reduction puts the guitar signal well into the amp's clean headroom.

**2. 6550 Cold-Bias HIGHER Headroom**
The 6550 tubes at 600V plate voltage have SIGNIFICANTLY more clean headroom than stock EL34s at 480V:
- EL34 at 480V begins to clip at approximately 30Vrms output (~25W clean)
- 6550 at 600V begins to clip at approximately 45-50Vrms output (~60-70W clean)
- This means the modified amps had roughly 3-4dB MORE clean headroom than stock Marshalls
- With guitar volume rolled back, the signal never reaches the 6550's clipping threshold
- The cold bias (Class B) would still contribute subtle crossover distortion at the zero-crossing point, but this is much less audible at lower signal levels

**3. Tone Stack Mid Emphasis**
The 33K slope resistor + 500pF treble cap shifts the mid center down from ~800Hz to ~600-700Hz. This gives the clean tone a warmer, fuller character than a stock Marshall -- more body, less harsh upper-mid presence.

**Net result**: The amps on "Changes" are operating almost entirely in their clean region, with perhaps the faintest whisper of crossover distortion from the Class B bias adding "life" to the tone. The midrange warmth comes from both the tone stack mods and the guitar's volume/tone knob settings.

### What About the Slight Breakup?

On transient attacks (hard-picked notes, chord accents), you can hear the faintest hint of tube "hair" -- a microsecond of harmonic distortion on the attack that immediately decays to clean. This is consistent with:
- The amp operating just below its clean ceiling
- Hard transients momentarily pushing into the clipping region
- The attack clipping while the sustain portion remains clean
- This "touch sensitivity" is characteristic of a well-biased tube amp operating near its breakup threshold

This is NOT fuzz or overdrive -- it's pure amp character. It's the musical hallmark of a cranked tube amp being played softly.

### Marshall 1960 4x12 Cabinets
- 6 cabinets total (mix of 1960A slant + 1960B straight)
- **Speakers**: Most likely 75-watt Rola replacements (from Thomas Organ) or JBL D120F

  - The G12H-30 is a possibility but less likely for the BOG era (Hendrix had already moved beyond those)

### Speaker Character on "Changes"
For the clean "Changes" tone, the speaker type matters LESS than on high-gain material because:
- The speakers are not being driven into breakup (signal level is moderate)
- The primary tonal coloring comes from the cabinet's resonant frequency and baffle diffraction, not speaker distortion
- A JBL D120F clean would sound very "transparent" and "hi-fi"
- A Celestion G12H-30 clean would sound slightly warmer with a gentle presence peak around 3-4kHz
- Since "Changes" is mostly sub-3kHz content (due to guitar tone/volume rolloff), the difference is subtle

## 6. PLAYING TECHNIQUE CONTEXT

### What Gives "Changes" Its Distinctive Character

The warm, round, slightly compressed character of "Changes" comes from a combination of factors, NONE of which are effects pedals:

**1. Neck Pickup Selection**
The neck pickup on a Strat produces the warmest, roundest tone available. The pickup is positioned where string vibration amplitude is greatest, emphasizing fundamental frequencies and lower harmonics. On a reversed left-handed Strat, the neck pickup's slant further warms the treble strings.

**2. Guitar Volume at ~5**
This does three things simultaneously:
- Reduces signal level ~14dB, keeping the amp clean
- The 250K pot acts as a variable treble bleed resistor -- at lower settings, high frequencies are shunted to ground through the pot's resistance in parallel with cable capacitance
- Creates a natural high-frequency shelf rolloff starting around 2-2.5kHz

**3. Guitar Tone at ~6**
Additional low-pass filtering through the .022uF tone cap circuit. At position 6, this creates a gentle rolloff starting around 2kHz with a slope of approximately 6dB/octave.

**4. Coiled Cable Capacitance**
Hendrix used long coiled cables (estimated 20-30 feet). At ~30pF/foot, this adds 600-900pF of capacitance loading on the pickup. Combined with the neck pickup's inductance (~6-8H for a vintage Strat neck pickup), this forms an LC resonant circuit with a peak around 2-2.5kHz that rolls off steeply above.

**5. Detuning to D Standard**
Lower tuning = longer vibrational wavelength = more fundamental content relative to harmonics. The .010-.038 strings at D tuning have very low tension, producing a warmer, bassier fundamental with less high-harmonic "zing."

**6. Soft Picking Dynamics**
"Changes" is played with a gentle touch -- fingertips and soft pick strokes. Harder picking excites more high harmonics; softer picking emphasizes the fundamental. This is a performance technique, not something we can preset.

**7. The Chord Voicings**
Hendrix uses jazz-influenced chord shapes on "Changes" -- extensions (7ths, 9ths), partial barre chords, and thumb-over-neck voicings. These spread notes across a wider frequency range than simple power chords, creating a richer, more complex harmonic tapestry that sounds inherently "warmer" and more sophisticated.

**8. Subtle Uni-Vibe Animation**
The Uni-Vibe in chorus mode at slow speed adds just enough phase-shift animation to give the clean tone "life" -- preventing it from sounding sterile or flat. This is the difference between "clean" and "beautiful clean."

### Combined Spectral Profile
All these factors create a signal that is:
- Rich below 1kHz (strong fundamentals from neck pickup + detuning)
- Warm 1-2kHz (midrange body from tone stack mods + guitar tone knob)
- Gentle rolloff 2-3kHz (combined cable capacitance + volume pot + tone pot)
- Virtually no content above 4kHz (all LP effects compound)
- Subtle phase animation from Uni-Vibe adds spatial dimension without pitch wobble
- Transient "hair" from amp tube compression on attack (sub-millisecond)

## 11. SONIC GAPS AND LIMITATIONS

### What We CAN Capture:
- Warm neck-pickup Strat character (via low inputGain + amp EQ)
- Subtle Uni-Vibe chorus animation
- Marshall Plexi clean-to-edge-of-breakup character
- Mid-forward EQ from tone stack mods (via amp EQ controls)
- Flat/neutral cabinet response (G12-65 IR)

### What We CANNOT Capture:
1. **Passive guitar electronics interaction** - The volume/tone pot interaction with cable capacitance creates a unique LP filter. Our inputGain is a simple level control, not a passive RC network simulation. The frequency-dependent attenuation is missing.
2. **Class B crossover distortion** - Our processTriode() does not model the zero-crossing notch from cold-biased 6550 tubes. At clean levels this is subtle but still contributes "life."
3. **Touch sensitivity** - The real amp responds differently to each pick stroke's attack velocity. Our amp model has some dynamic response but not the full tube compression envelope.
4. **String tension / detuning character** - Lower tuning affects harmonic ratios at the source; we can only simulate the result through EQ.
5. **Hendrix's picking technique** - Soft, fingertip-heavy attack angle that emphasizes fundamentals.
6. **Room ambience** - The Fillmore East's acoustics contribute to the recording's character.

### Workaround for Gap #1 (Passive Guitar Electronics):
The frequency-dependent volume rolloff could be approximated by adding a subtle low-pass EQ cut:
- ParametricEQ (index 7): Enable with a gentle shelf at -3dB above 2.5kHz
- This simulates the combined treble loss from volume pot + tone pot + cable capacitance
- However, this adds another enabled effect to what should be a minimalist chain

Alternative: Reduce AmpModel treble slightly further (0.35 instead of 0.40) to compensate within the amp block itself.
